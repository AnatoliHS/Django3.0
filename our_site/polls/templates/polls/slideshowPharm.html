    What is WHIMIS

    What is WHIMIS

    WHMIS:

    The Workplace Hazardous Materials Information System (WHMIS) is Canada‚Äôs national hazard communication standard for workplaces. It was originally established in 1988 and updated in 2015 to align with the Globally Harmonized System (GHS) of classification and labelling of chemicals. WHMIS ensures that employers and workers receive consistent and comprehensive safety information about any hazardous products they might encounter at work. By setting standardized requirements for classifying hazards and communicating them through labels and Safety Data Sheets (SDSs), WHMIS helps reduce workplace injuries and illnesses related to dangerous chemicals. In a pharmacy, where various chemicals and materials are used daily, WHMIS is crucial to keep staff informed about product dangers and precautions.



    • Product Classification ‚Äì Hazardous products are categorized by their properties (e.g. flammable, toxic, corrosive) so that appropriate caution can be taken.

    • Labels ‚Äì Containers of hazardous products must have labels providing basic hazard information to workers in a standardized format.

    • Safety Data Sheets (SDSs) ‚Äì Detailed information sheets that supplement labels with in-depth data about the product‚Äôs properties, risks, and safety precautions.

    • Worker Education and Training ‚Äì Employers must educate and train workers to understand labels and SDSs, and to apply this knowledge on the job.

    What is WHIMIS

    WHMIS legislation includes:

    • Workplace safety, including WHMIS, is a shared responsibility between federal and provincial governments. WHMIS 2015 (the current system) is implemented through complementary federal rules (which focus on suppliers) and provincial rules (which focus on employers and workplaces). In Ontario, WHMIS requirements are enforced under the Occupational Health and Safety Act (OHSA) and the WHMIS Regulation (R.R.O. 1990, Reg. 860).

    • Federal WHMIS requirements: Under federal law (the Hazardous Products Act), suppliers (manufacturers, importers, distributors) of hazardous products must classify their products and provide proper labels and SDSs when selling or importing for workplace use.

    • Provincial (Ontario) WHMIS requirements: Under Ontario‚Äôs OHSA and Reg. 860, employers have specific duties to protect workers from hazardous products. In general, Ontario employers must:


      • Ensure identification of hazardous products: Every hazardous chemical or material in the workplace must be clearly identified (usually by an appropriate WHMIS label).

      • Obtain and maintain SDSs: Employers must have an up-to-date Safety Data Sheet for each hazardous product and make SDSs readily accessible to workers.

      • Provide education and training: Employers must develop a WHMIS program that educates workers on the hazards of products in their workplace and trains them on safe use, handling, storage, and emergency procedures.S

    Responsibilities under WHIMIS
    Responsibilities under WHIMIS

    Employer:

    In a pharmacy, the employer (or owner/manager) has the primary duty to establish and maintain a safe environment regarding hazardous materials. Under WHMIS in Ontario, key employer responsibilities include:

    • Identify and Inventory Hazardous Products: Keep an up-to-date inventory of all hazardous chemicals and materials in the clinic (e.g., disinfectants, chemical cleaners, mercury, etc.)
    • Obtain and Make Available SDSs: For every hazardous product in the clinic, obtain a current Safety Data Sheet from the supplier and keep it accessible (for example, in a binder or an electronic database that all staff can access).
    • Provide Education and Training: Employers must develop a WHMIS education and training program for workers.
    • Ensure Controlled Products Are Properly Labelled: Employers must not allow hazardous products to be used if they are not properly labelled.
    • Implement Safe Work Practices and Controls: Employers need to establish procedures and controls to reduce the risk from hazardous materials.
    • Consult and Review: In Ontario, employers should consult the Joint Health and Safety Committee or Health and Safety Representative (if applicable in the pharmacy office) about the WHMIS training program and review the program‚Äôs effectiveness at least annually.
    Responsibilities under WHIMIS

    Employee:

    Worker (Employee) Responsibilities: pharmacy staff also have responsibilities under WHMIS. Workers are not passive recipients of information ‚Äì they must actively apply what they learn and follow safe practices. Key employee responsibilities include:

    • Participate in Training: Workers must attend WHMIS training sessions provided by the employer and pay attention so they understand the hazards of products in the clinic. Training is not a one-time event; workers should also participate in refreshers and new training whenever procedures or products change.
    • Read and Follow Labels and SDSs: Before using any hazardous product (like a chemical disinfectant or a bottle of mercury capsule waste), workers should read the product‚Äôs label for hazard information and recommended precautions.
    • Use Recommended Protective Measures: It is the worker‚Äôs responsibility to follow the safety procedures established by the employer.
    • Handle and Store Materials Safely: Workers should take care when handling hazardous materials ‚Äì use caution to avoid spills or exposure, do not mix chemicals unless instructed, keep containers closed when not in use, etc.
    • Report Issues: If a worker finds a container with no label or an illegible label, or if they notice any hazard that isn‚Äôt properly controlled (like a leaking chemical container or broken ventilation), they should report it to their supervisor immediately.
    • Respond to Emergencies as Trained: In the event of a spill, exposure, or other incident, workers must act according to their training.

    CATEGORIES

Within a category, Subcategory 1A is more hazardous than 1B, and so on. Some classes use types instead of categories, where Type A is more hazardous than Type B, and this pattern continues as well.

    In the Acute Toxicity class, substances are divided into:

    • Category 1: Fatal or toxic when inhaled, ingested, or absorbed
    • Category 2: Toxic but not as severe as Category 1.
    • Category 3: Harmful but not fatal.

    Similarly, in the Carcinogenicity hazard class:

    • Category 1A: Known to cause cancer in humans.
    • Category 1B: Presumed to cause cancer in humans.
    • Category 2: Suspected of causing cancer.

    WHEN IS WORKPLACE LABEL REQUIRED

    • Transfer/Decanting: If you pour or transfer a hazardous product from its original container into another container (for example, putting concentrated cleaner into a secondary bottle, or mixing a solution in a beaker), and the product will not be used immediately by the person who did the transfer, then the new container needs a workplace label.

    What is an SDS and Why is it Important?

    A Safety Data Sheet (SDS) ‚Äì formerly called a Material Safety Data Sheet (MSDS) in WHMIS 1988 ‚Äì is essentially a fact sheet or technical document that provides detailed information about a hazardous product. Think of it as the encyclopedia entry for the chemical. The SDS is prepared by the product‚Äôs manufacturer or supplier according to strict requirements in the Hazardous Products Regulations. It supplements the label by giving the reasoning and deeper details behind those label warnings.


    Safety Data Sheets (SDS)

    Why do you Need SDS?

    While labels provide a snapshot of key information, the Safety Data Sheet (SDS) is where you find the full story about a hazardous product. An SDS is a comprehensive document that outlines everything from a chemical‚Äôs ingredients to its fire-fighting measures to its toxicological data.


    Safety Data Sheets (SDS)

    PURPOSE OF SDS

    • It lists the hazards of the product in detail (both physical and health hazards, and environmental hazards too).
    • It describes safe handling and storage recommendations beyond the short phrases on the label.
    • It provides first aid measures, fire-fighting measures, and spill response procedures.
    • It gives toxicological data (how poisonous or irritating a substance is, effects of exposure).
    • It may include regulatory information, like if the product is controlled under certain laws, and whether any ingredients are on hazardous lists.
    • It is a key resource for emergency responders (firefighters, doctors treating exposure, etc.) because it contains specifics like flash point, chemical formula, reactivity info, antidotes, etc.
    Safety Data Sheets (SDS)

    WHEN SHOULD YOU CONSULT AN SDS

    • When you start using a new product, read its SDS to familiarize yourself with all hazards and precautions (even beyond what training covered).
    • If you are going to do something out of the ordinary with a product (e.g., heat it, dilute it significantly, or use it in a way not covered in training).
    • When a spill or exposure occurs, and you need detailed first aid or cleanup instructions.
    • If you or someone in the clinic has a health condition or sensitivity ‚Äì the SDS might indicate if certain people should be extra cautious (for instance, some SDSs say ‚Äúmedical conditions aggravated by exposure‚Äù).
    • Periodically during safety meetings or refreshers, reviewing SDSs can reinforce knowledge.
    • If you simply want to know more about a chemical (e.g., ‚ÄúWhat exactly is in this cold sterilant solution? Why does it smell this way? What is its pH?‚Äù).

    Section 2
    Hazard Identification:
    Here you‚Äôll find the WHMIS classification of the product (which hazard classes it falls under) and the label elements ‚Äì basically what pictograms, signal word, hazard statements apply, plus any other hazard not otherwise classified.     If it‚Äôs a single substance, this section gives the chemical name, formula, CAS number, etc. If it‚Äôs a mixture, it lists hazardous ingredients and their concentrations (ranges) in the mixture.     Section 5
    Fire-Fighting Measures:
    Information on what to do if the product catches fire. It lists suitable extinguishing media (water, CO‚ÇÇ, foam, dry chemical, etc.), any specific hazards from fire (like ‚Äúmay produce toxic fumes of chlorine‚Äù or ‚Äúcontainers may explode in heat‚Äù), and special protective equipment for firefighters (like wear self-contained breathing apparatus). For example, if an SDS says ‚ÄúDo not use water jet on burning liquid‚Äù you‚Äôd know to use an extinguisher instead of water.     Section 6
    Accidental Release Measures:
    How to safely clean up spills or leaks. For instance, if a mercury spill occurs, the SDS will say use a mercury spill kit or sulfur powder, and not to use a vacuum. Or if you drop a bottle of acid, SDS might say neutralize with sodium bicarbonate then mop up. Following these instructions protects you and others from harm during cleanup.
    SECTIONS OF SDS
    Section 7
    Handling and Storage:
    Guidelines for safe use and storage. It might repeat some precautions (like ‚Äúuse in fume hood‚Äù or ‚Äúkeep away from heat, sparks‚Äù), and tells you storage conditions (temperature range, keep out of sunlight, keep container tightly closed, incompatible materials to avoid storing near). _Why it matters:_ It helps you set up your storage correctly ‚Äì e.g., store this chemical in a cool dark cabinet, or do not store acid and bleach together (incompatibility).     Section 8
    Exposure Controls/Personal Protection:
    This section is very practical.     Section 9
    Physical and Chemical Properties:
    This is a list of the product‚Äôs physical properties: appearance (color, physical state), odor (and odor threshold), pH, melting/freezing point, boiling point, flash point (for flammables), evaporation rate, flammability limits, vapor pressure, vapor density, relative density (specific gravity), solubility, partition coefficient, auto-ignition temp, decomposition temp, and viscosity. Why it matters:_ For a user, some of these are interesting but not critical.

    Section 10
    Stability and Reactivity:
    Details on how stable the product is and what conditions to avoid. It lists reactivity (is it reactive with water? etc.), chemical stability (stable under normal conditions or not), conditions to avoid (heat, shock, light, etc.), incompatible materials (what other chemicals cause dangerous reactions ‚Äì e.g., ‚ÄúAvoid contact with strong acids, oxidizers‚Äù), and hazardous decomposition products (like if it burns or decays, does it produce CO, NOx, chlorine gas, etc.).
